Entry-Level Job Analysis in Zimbabwe

Status: Completed
Category: Data Analytics
Scope: National Market Analysis

An analysis of the Zimbabwean job market to determine the careers with the highest demand and the relationship between a location in the country and the most common job positions posted by businesses.

Project Overview & Objectives

Navigating the entry-level job ecosystem can be difficult due to fragmented job postings and unstructured hiring patterns. The primary objective of this case study was to programmatically scrape, clean, and map hiring requests across different cities in Zimbabwe. This data mining loop uncovers hidden trends regarding what industries are expanding and where specific tech and business talents are concentrated.

The Technical Pipeline

To achieve a reliable analytical framework, the data was processed through three distinct operational phases:

  • Ingestion & Scraping: Target acquisition routines collected raw hiring listings from regional bulletin feeds, job boards, and business indexes.
  • Data Transformation (Pandas): Handled string isolation filters to fix misspelled job titles, eliminated duplicate records, and normalized location coordinates (e.g., merging variations of "Harare" and "Bulawayo").
  • Spatial Clustering: Evaluated position dense nodes against geographic markers to determine if business layout requirements vary by province.

Key Analytical Discoveries

The findings show a clear link between a business's physical location and its functional hiring demands:

  1. The Regional Core Split: Technical roles, full-stack programming positions, and operational administration management tasks dominate the corporate job market in Harare. Meanwhile, logistics, supply chain tracking, and agricultural processing roles appear more frequently in regional hubs.
  2. The Entry-Level Skills Gap: A high percentage of entry-level business and technical postings specifically require cloud literacy, basic database interaction tools (SQL), or data management skills, signaling a shift in baseline hiring expectations.
